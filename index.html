A little bit about me...


The Best Way to Predict the Future is to Invent it!

-Allan Kay



Hi, I'm Clayton — hacker, developer, and prospective engineer. I am currently studying mechatronics engineering at McMaster University. I have a passion for both hardware and software and my goal is to advance embedded technology by futher integrating these two fields.


In my spare time I enjoy tinkering with microcontrollers such as Arduino, STM32F Discovery boards, and Linux BeagleBoards. Currently I am attempting to automate aspects of my house using an Arduino and parts that I have machined and 3D printed.


I also enjoy practicing my coding skills. Although I have experience using a variety of tools (C, C++ Matlab, Labview, etc.), I am most comfortable with Java and Python.


Projects

Push the limits...

  • January - March 2015

    Sumobot Competition

    I enteded an autonomous robotics competition where competing bots had to knock each other out of a ring. My contributions included the blueprint and construction of the chassis as well as design of the attack/avoid algorithm.

  • Feb 2015

    Signals and Systems

    I was tasked with the design and implementation of a digital circuit which would display a binary represenation of an varying alalog voltage. The device would "count up" to the voltage display using a DAC, a series of flip-flops, a clock and a latch. The circuit was designed and simulated in National Instrument's MultiSIM before being successfully constructed in the lab.

  • Sept 2014

    Java Calculator with GUI

    Using the eclipse IDE and JUnit for debugging, I constructed a calculator in Java with a graphical front end. The calculator used a basic hash table for storage and could switch between integer and floating-point mode.

  • December 2013

    "Engineering Design Competition"

    In a month-long design challange, our team of four designed a device to help a patient with rhumatoid arthritis. This crippling disease makes even the most basic tasks extremely painful and difficult so we chose to focus one one basic task that would allow the patient to remain somewhat independant—pumping gasoline.
